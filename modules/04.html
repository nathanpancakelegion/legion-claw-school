Module 04: Building Your Agent Team

Paid

From Solo Agent to Full Team

You've got one agent running and handling a real task. Now we scale. The mistake most people make is trying to build one "super agent" that does everything. That's like hiring one employee and making them your salesperson, bookkeeper, researcher, and content writer — they'll be mediocre at all of it. The better approach: specialized agents, each with a clear job. Just like a real team.

The AI Org Chart

Here's the agent team structure I recommend for any small business or startup. You don't need all of these on day one, but this is what you're building toward. Recon — Research & Intelligence Agent: Researches companies, people, markets, and competitors. Triggers before meetings, when entering a new market, or evaluating a deal. Produces structured briefings, competitive analyses, and market reports. Ghost — Content & Communications Agent: Drafts emails, social posts, blog content, presentations, and internal docs. Triggers when you need written content in your voice. Ops — Operations & Admin Agent: Organizes information, tracks deliverables, processes data, maintains records. Triggers after meetings, when processing documents, and for weekly admin tasks. Closer — Sales & Outreach Agent: Drafts outreach, follow-up sequences, pitch personalization, and objection handling. Triggers when prospecting, following up, or preparing for sales conversations. Sentinel — QA & Review Agent: Reviews other agents' output for quality, consistency, and accuracy. Triggers before anything goes external — emails to prospects, published content, client deliverables.

Why Specialization Works

When you give one agent a super long, complex instruction set covering research AND sales AND operations AND content, it loses focus on any individual task, produces generic output, can't maintain different tones for different contexts, and gets harder to debug when something goes wrong. Specialized agents have shorter and more focused Role Cards, consistent output quality for their specific domain, clear ownership — when research is wrong you fix Recon, not the entire system — and different tones and styles appropriate to their function.

Building Agent #2: The Walkthrough

Let's build your second agent using Ghost (content agent) as the example. Step 1 — Write the Role Card using RICO: "You are Ghost, my content and communications agent. You write in my voice — direct, professional, occasionally informal. You understand my business and my audience. You never use corporate buzzwords, filler phrases, or fluff." For Input, you'll provide the type of content needed, the audience, key points or source material, and any constraints. For Constraints: always match your voice, never write anything longer than requested, no emojis unless specified, end every email with a clear next step or CTA, and if there's not enough information to write something good, ask before producing a weak draft. Step 2 — Upload Knowledge Files: 5-10 examples of emails you've written that represent your voice, your company one-pager, brand guidelines, and a list of phrases you love and hate. Step 3 — Create the Claude Project with the Role Card as Custom Instructions. Step 4 — Calibrate by running at least 5 different tasks through Ghost and updating the Role Card with every correction.

Agent Coordination: The Handoff System

Here's where the real power emerges — agents handing off to each other. Example workflow for an investor meeting: You tell Recon to research Zeal Capital Partners and Leo Peyronnin. Recon produces a briefing document. You paste that briefing into Closer to draft a personalized agenda and three talking points. After the meeting, you paste your notes into Ops to process into action items, follow-up tasks, and a CRM update. You hand the follow-up tasks to Ghost to write a thank-you email. Before sending, you paste Ghost's email into Sentinel to review for tone, accuracy, and any claims you can't back up. Five agents, one workflow, 20 minutes instead of 2 hours. Right now you're manually passing information between agents — that's fine. In Module 08, we'll discuss automation options that reduce even this overhead.

How Many Agents Do You Need?

Start with two. Get them both dialed in over one week. Then add a third. Recommended build order: First agent — whatever your highest-volume recurring task is (you built this in Module 02). Second — Ghost (content), because almost everyone writes content regularly. Third — Recon (research), because good intel makes everything else better. Fourth and fifth — Ops and Closer, based on which you need more. By the end of this course, you'll have all five. But don't rush it. A well-calibrated team of two agents outperforms a poorly configured team of ten.
