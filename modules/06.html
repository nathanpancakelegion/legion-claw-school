Module 06: Automating Operations & Admin

Paid

The Admin Tax

Every business owner pays an invisible tax: the hours spent on meeting notes, status updates, data entry, file organization, and follow-up tracking. It's not strategic work. It doesn't generate revenue. But it has to get done or things fall apart. This is where your Ops agent earns its keep. Ops handles the organizational backbone of your business so you can focus on the work that actually moves the needle.

Building Ops: Your Operations Agent

Here's Ops' full Role Card. Role: "You are Ops, my operations and administration agent. You process unstructured information into organized, actionable formats. You are meticulous, consistent, and efficient. You never summarize away important details — you restructure them. You think in systems: inputs, processes, outputs." Input: One of five types — Meeting Notes (raw notes or transcripts), Task Processing (items that need organizing, prioritizing, or tracking), Data Cleanup (messy data that needs reformatting), Status Request (a request for a status summary), or Document Processing (a document to parse, summarize, or restructure). Constraints: Never lose information during processing — restructure, don't reduce. Flag ambiguities rather than making assumptions. Use consistent formatting. When assigning task owners, use names provided — if no owner is clear, flag as "Owner: TBD." Date everything. If a task has a deadline, bold it.

Ops Template 1: Meeting Notes Processor

When you dump raw meeting notes into Ops, it produces a structured document: Meeting date and attendees, meeting type (Internal/External/Investor/Partner), Key Decisions as a bullet list, Action Items in a table with task, owner, deadline, and status columns, Open Questions that weren't resolved, Follow-Up Required with who needs to do what by when, and the Raw Notes preserved at the bottom for reference. This turns a mess of bullet points and partial sentences into something you can actually act on.

Ops Template 2: Weekly Status Report

Feed Ops your week's activities and it produces: Completed This Week (items with brief context), In Progress as a table with item, status, next step, and blocker columns, Priorities for Next Week ranked with why each matters, Decisions Needed with who needs to make them and by when, and Metrics if applicable showing this week vs. last week with trend indicators. This replaces the 30-60 minutes most people spend manually compiling their weekly status.

Ops Template 3: Task Prioritization

Give Ops a brain dump of everything on your plate and it organizes using the Eisenhower matrix: Urgent + Important (Do Now) with deadlines and time estimates, Important + Not Urgent (Schedule) with target dates, Urgent + Not Important (Delegate) with delegation targets, and Neither (Drop or Defer) with reasoning for why each can wait. It finishes with a Recommended Focus Order for today — your top 3 tasks ranked with why each is first.

Ops Template 4: Data Cleanup

When you have messy data — a CSV disaster, a spreadsheet with inconsistent formatting, contact information scattered across emails — Ops structures it. Feed it a pile of contacts from a conference and it produces a clean table with Name, Company, Title, Email, Phone, Notes, and Follow-Up columns. It flags Missing Information (who's missing an email, whose company is unclear) and suggests Groupings — Hot Leads, Potential Partners, General Network — with reasoning for each.

Real-World Workflow: Post-Meeting Processing

Here's the actual post-meeting workflow: Meeting ends, you have rough notes (bullet points, partial sentences, voice-to-text transcription — messy). Paste the raw notes into Ops: "Process these meeting notes. Attendees were these people. This was this type of meeting." Ops produces structured meeting notes with action items, decisions, and follow-ups. Review for 2 minutes, make corrections. Paste the action items into your task tracker. Hand the follow-up items to Ghost for email drafting. Total time: 5 minutes. Previously: 20-30 minutes of organizing after every meeting.

Connecting Ops to Your Other Agents

Ops is the connective tissue of your AI workforce. Recon's research output gets filed and tracked through Ops. Meeting notes flow through Ops to Ghost for follow-up emails. Ops identifies which follow-ups are sales-related and queues them for Closer. Any agent output that needs to be logged, tracked, or organized goes through Ops. Think of Ops as your Chief of Staff — it doesn't make strategic decisions, but it makes sure everything is documented, organized, and nothing falls through the cracks.

Automation Level-Up: Zapier Integration

For those ready to go further, Ops becomes truly powerful when connected to Zapier. Forward certain emails to a Zapier workflow that sends them to Ops for processing. After each calendar meeting, auto-generate a meeting notes template. When someone fills out a form, Ops processes and categorizes the submission. We'll cover this more in Module 08, but start thinking about which Ops tasks could be triggered automatically.
